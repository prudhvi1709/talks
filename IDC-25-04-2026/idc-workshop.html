Welcome everyone. This is not a session where you watch me build something and then clap at the end. This is a working session where you build something and ship it. My name is Prudhvi. I’m a Data Scientist at Gramener, and I spend a lot of my time building practical AI workflows and prototypes. But today is not really about my background. Today is about making sure each of you builds something real and shares a link before we leave. The bar today is not production-ready. The bar is one real link that does one useful, interesting, or funny thing. If it is ugly but it works, that still counts as a win. I want you to remember one constraint through the whole workshop: one user, one input, one output. The fastest way to fail in a two-hour session is to make the idea too ambitious. Keep these slides open. I also have backup tools ready if anything breaks. If you get stuck later, I will help you cut scope, simplify the app, and get you to a working version.

One Model Is Not The Whole Story

  • 1 What LLM do you use most today?
  • 2 What coding agent do you reach for first?
  • 3 When one model gets stuck, what do you switch to?
  • 4 Which tool do you trust for coding, research, and presenting?
Use this as a framing pause, not as a poll that needs a long discussion. The point is simple: do not build your mental model of AI around one product. Different tools are better at different kinds of work, and part of building well is knowing when to switch. I am not asking people to become experts in every model. I am asking them to notice that coding, research, and presentation-making do not always reward the same tool. That matters in this workshop because if one path feels blocked, the right move is often not to push harder. The right move is to simplify, switch tools, or change the task shape.

Idea Accelerators

Don't build a generic chatbot. Pick one messy input, one sharp user, one surprising output.

Use Your Own Data

  • Bank statement → money story
  • Calendar + deadlines → student cockpit
  • Screen time / health logs → behavior mirror

Use A Live Signal

  • Camera → posture or drowsiness coach
  • Voice / journal → dream or mood mapper
  • Location / meetings → timely assistant

Make It Weird Enough To Share

  • Text / diary → soundtrack or trailer
  • Group chat → roast or memory map
  • Unread PDF → visual explainer

Best prompts start with: "Take this input, do one smart thing, return something I can use or share."

This is where I want to push you away from generic chatbot ideas and toward specific transformations. A good idea for today looks like this: take one messy input, do one smart thing, and return one output that is useful or shareable. If you are stuck, ask yourself three questions. What data do I already have? What live signal can I capture? What would be useful enough or funny enough to share in the group today? If your idea still feels vague, shrink it aggressively. Do not build a career assistant. Build a resume bullet improver. Do not build a study platform. Build a PDF-to-quiz generator. Do not build a finance advisor. Build a bank CSV to spending categories app. For today, one screen, one flow, and one output is enough.

Five Things That Actually Help

  • 1 Plan before you prompt. Three sentences about what you're building saves 30 minutes of fixing.
  • 2 One thing at a time. One big prompt breaks everything. Three small ones build something.
  • 3 Read what it generates. Code that looks right and code that is right look identical. Until they don't.
  • 4 Test before adding more. The whack-a-mole problem is real. Build 20 lines, test, then continue.
  • 5 Imagination is now the bottleneck. Not your ability to code. Knowing what to build is the hard part.
These are the rules that will keep the room moving. First, plan before you prompt. Spend three minutes writing down what the app does before you ask an AI tool to generate anything. Second, do one thing at a time. One giant prompt usually creates a giant mess. Small steps are slower for one minute and faster for the next hour. Third, read the code and test it before you add more. Build a small piece, run it, check it, and only then move on. If you need a prompt template, use this: build a very small web app for this user, take this input, return this output, keep it single-page, keep the UI minimal, skip auth, skip database unless absolutely necessary, make the core flow work first, and then stop for review.

The Build

Round 1
15 min
Pick the idea. Scope it down.
One user. One input. One output. Start smaller than you want.
Round 2
25 min
Get version one working.
Ugly is fine. Broken edges are fine. Make the core loop work.
Round 3
20 min
Add one thing that feels smart.
Use your data, a live signal, or a better output. Not all three.
Round 4
20 min
Polish, test, publish.
Fix obvious bugs. Add a short intro. Get a link you can share confidently.
Final 20 Min
Demo, debrief, Q&A.
Share links. Talk about what worked, what broke, what surprised you, and what you would do next.
This is the structure for the build window, and I want you to follow it instead of improvising your own timeline. In round one, pick the idea and cut scope. Before you prompt anything, write the app in three sentences: who it is for, what goes in, and what comes out. In round two, get version one working. Ignore polish. Ignore branding. Ignore fancy extras. Just make the core loop work. In round three, add one thing that feels smart. One better output, one better prompt, one real dataset, or one useful visualization. Not all of them. In round four, polish, test, and publish. Add a title, add one-line description, make sure it works, and get the link ready to share. If your build keeps failing, cut aggressively: remove auth, remove the database, remove extra pages, hardcode sample data if needed, and rebuild around the simplest working flow.

End on the participants, not on me. The point of this workshop is that ordinary people walked into this room and shipped something real in one session. That is the proof. That is what matters. The big takeaway is not that AI magically builds everything for you. The real takeaway is that AI reduces build friction, but judgment still matters. Scope discipline is still the difference between shipping and stalling. The people who ship fastest are usually not the people with the biggest ideas. They are the people who choose the simplest idea that can still be shared. Let’s look at what got built, what broke, what surprised you, and what you would do next if you had one more hour.
